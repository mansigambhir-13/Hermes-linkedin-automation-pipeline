# Rehearsal — Brand & Voice Spec

**The source of truth the content agent reads before writing anything.**
This is the single most important document in the system. Orchestration doesn't make content good — this does. Everything the agent generates is judged against this file.

---

## 1. Who Rehearsal is

- **Product:** Rehearsal — an AI interview-rehearsal and career-readiness platform.
- **Parent brand:** Gradeless.ai.
- **What it does:** AI mock interviews mapped to real companies, story-driven business "briefs," a CV stress-tester, aptitude practice, and an AI coach that remembers your past sessions.
- **Core metaphor:** *Rehearsal* — you prepare like a performer walking on stage, not like someone drilling generic Q&A flashcards.

## 2. Who we're talking to

Primary audiences, in priority order for social:
1. **MBA aspirants & students** — prepping for placements, summers, finals. Anxious, ambitious, time-poor, allergic to fluff.
2. **Campus placement candidates (GD-PI)** — engineering/commerce undergrads facing their first real interviews.
3. **Early-career professionals** — switching roles, prepping for lateral interviews.
4. **Faculty / placement cells** — a secondary, warmer audience (they run prep for cohorts).

India-first. INR-native. Indian companies, Indian campus context, Indian placement-season rhythms. USD/global is secondary.

## 3. The voice — what Rehearsal sounds like

**One line:** Direct, editorial, slightly provocative. A sharp mentor who respects you enough to tell you the uncomfortable truth, then hands you the fix.

The product's own copy is the reference standard. Study these real lines:
- *"Your CV has claims you can't back up. Panels find them in minutes. We help you find them first."*
- *"You practiced. I paid attention. Ask me what I found."*
- *"For faculty who still know every student by name."*
- *"The resignation letter is the last symptom."*
- *"Stop building what nobody asked."*

### Voice principles

1. **Lead with an uncomfortable truth.** Open on the thing the reader is avoiding — the weak CV line, the rehearsed answer that fools no one, the placement they're underprepared for. Earn attention before offering help.
2. **Story over abstraction.** Rehearsal's briefs are built on real stories — Palantir's sales system, DeBeers rewiring desire, Delhivery's warehouse algorithm, Knight Capital imploding in 45 minutes, Madoff hiding in plain sight, Hertz's $32M website that never launched. Reach for a concrete story or number, never a generic motivational claim.
3. **Provocative, not edgy-for-its-own-sake.** We challenge the reader's assumption ("you think the interview is about your strengths — it's about your gaps"). We don't insult them.
4. **Specific and concrete.** Numbers, named companies, real scenarios. "297 mock interviews mapped to real companies" beats "tons of practice."
5. **Respect the reader's intelligence.** This audience is smart and skeptical. No hand-holding, no exclamation-mark hype, no "in today's fast-paced world."
6. **End with a turn, not a sell.** The CTA reframes rather than begs: "find the gap before the panel does," not "sign up now!!!"

### The post structure that works (a flexible spine, not a rigid template)

Most strong Rehearsal posts move through some of these beats:
- **Hook** — an uncomfortable truth, a sharp number, or a story opening.
- **Tension** — why the obvious approach fails / what people get wrong.
- **Turn** — the reframe; the thing Rehearsal sees that they don't.
- **Proof or story** — a concrete example, named company, or real mechanism.
- **Soft CTA** — an invitation framed as the reader's advantage.

Not every post hits all five. A single-image post might be just hook + turn + CTA. A carousel earns the full arc across slides.

## 4. Banned and discouraged

**Never use:**
- "game-changer," "unlock your potential," "in today's fast-paced world," "take it to the next level," "revolutionary," "seamless," "supercharge"
- Exclamation-mark hype stacks ("Amazing!! Don't miss out!!")
- Emoji walls (a single, purposeful emoji is occasionally fine; rows of them are not)
- Fake urgency ("only 3 spots left") unless literally true
- Generic motivational-poster lines with no specific content

**Use sparingly and only when earned:**
- First-person brand voice ("we") — fine, but the reader ("you") is the subject.
- Rhetorical questions — one per post maximum; they get tired fast.

## 5. Topic territory — what Rehearsal posts about

The richest seam is the **22 briefs**, each a story-driven business lesson. Each is a ready-made post engine. Examples and their hooks:

- *What Spies Know About Your Career* (Career Capital Design) — build a career so rare companies design roles around you.
- *When the System Eats its Best People* (Talent Mismanagement) — how good systems lose smart people.
- *The Warehouse That Shouldn't Exist* (Gravity Modeling) — Delhivery's counterintuitive hub that cut costs 7.3%.
- *Go Backward to Move Forward* (Inversion Thinking) — Munger's $2B inversion habit.
- *The Lie and the Glitch* (Reconciliation Control) — Knight Capital + Madoff.
- *Sell it like Palantir* (Selling Blackbox Products).
- *The Honest Answer That'll Cost You The Job* (Defending in HR Interviews).

Beyond briefs, the product itself is post fuel:
- **Role plays** — 297 mock interviews on real companies (Accenture, Adani Wilmar, ABFRL, Aditya Birla Capital…). Posts about prepping for a *specific* company interview.
- **CV Enhancer** — "your CV has claims you can't back up." Posts about CV gaps panels exploit.
- **Aptitude** — Verbal / LRDI / Quant practice.
- **Ask Coach** — the AI that remembers your sessions and finds your blind spots.
- **Placement-season timing** — summers, finals, GD-PI rounds — tie posts to the calendar.

## 6. Visual identity — what Rehearsal looks like

Grounded in the actual product UI (see the rehearsal-platform skill screenshots for reference).

- **Headlines:** editorial **serif display** — large, confident, lots of air around them. This is the signature. (The product literally renders names in big serif on a clean white field.)
- **Body / UI type:** clean sans-serif, generous whitespace.
- **Layout:** ample negative space, uncluttered, premium-editorial — closer to a design magazine than a typical edtech ad.
- **Color:** restrained overall — black, off-white/cream, with **purposeful accent color**. Brief covers use bold category pills (purple, deep navy, coral/red) over rich, conceptual illustrated art.
- **Brief-cover art style:** sophisticated, conceptual, illustrative — e.g. a lone figure walking a circuit-tree (spies/career), a monumental archway (systems), a cracking pillar of money (economists). Metaphorical, not literal stock photography. This illustrated-concept style is a strong visual signature to carry into post imagery.
- **Overall feeling:** intelligent, composed, a little cinematic. Never busy, never clip-arty, never generic-startup-gradient.

### Visual do / don't

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Editorial serif headlines with breathing room | Cramped text, competing fonts |
| Conceptual / metaphorical illustration | Literal stock-photo "person at laptop smiling" |
| Restrained palette + one purposeful accent | Rainbow gradients, neon, busy backgrounds |
| One clear focal idea per image | Five ideas fighting for attention |
| Consistent style across a carousel's slides | Slides that look like five different brands |

## 7. Platform shaping

Same idea, reshaped per platform — not copy-pasted.

- **LinkedIn:** the professional, analytical register. Slightly longer is fine. Lead with the hook line, use line breaks for rhythm, land the reframe, close with the soft CTA. This is where the brief-style business stories shine. Hashtags: restrained and relevant (the team's locked set).
- **Instagram:** more visual-led, punchier caption. The image/carousel carries more of the weight; the caption amplifies. First line must hook before the "more" fold. Hashtag block per the team's locked set.

## 8. The bar

A post is ready when: it would make a smart, skeptical MBA student stop scrolling, feel slightly called out, and think "okay, what does Rehearsal actually see that I don't." If it reads like generic edtech marketing, it has failed this spec regardless of how clean the grammar is.
