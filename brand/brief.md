## 1. Mission and strategic frame

### What Rehearsal is

Rehearsal is a mobile-first capsule-learning app. 15-minute interactive courses on management and business concepts, taught with documentary narration, interactive games, and cinematic visual storytelling. Two named products inside the app:

- **DeepProbe** — AI mock-interview engine that asks the third follow-up question a human coach wouldn't, evaluates with specificity, and grades on multiple dimensions.
- **Concept Briefs** — current-affairs and business-awareness microlearning (Indus Basin Treaty, ICICI IPO, electricity-market reform, Big Four firms, Karnataka bike-taxi policy, etc.) framed not as news but as defensible interview material.

Two primary audiences:

- **India (70% of the campaign weight):** MBA aspirants in the CAT → IIM PI → placement lifecycle. State: anxious, performance-pressured, surrounded by coaching products that don't actually move the needle.
- **West (30% as halo):** Business-fluency professionals who read Blinkist / Headway / Personal MBA / Finshots and feel informed but inarticulate in high-stakes meetings.

### The single wound the campaign weaponizes

> *"You're smart. You read. You can't articulate it under pressure."*

This wound is **dual-audience identity-mirror**. A 99.5%-ile IIM aspirant freezing in a PI panel and a senior PM who can't explain System 1 vs System 2 in a board meeting are emotionally the same person. The campaign exists to make that recognition impossible to avoid.

### The named enemies (use sparingly, never libelously)

1. **The Indian coaching industry** — sells once-a-month mocks for a daily anxiety. Doesn't name competitors directly unless quoting a public claim.
2. **Summary apps (Blinkist, Headway, Shortform, Sumizeit, BeFreed, StoryShots)** — "highlighters that don't make you smarter." Frame as a category, not as individual targets.
3. **Content-as-prep media (Finshots, InsideIIM)** — news without a practice layer. Frame as: "great content, but information ≠ articulation."

The brand never punches down on candidates or schools. It punches at categories and at industry assumptions.

## 2. Brand voice character

### One-line definition

> **Calm-authoritative documentary observer.** Third-person. Evidence-first. Has watched thousands of mock interviews and reports back what it saw.

### Closest brand reference points

- **A24** — cinematic, restrained, lets discomfort breathe
- **Linear** — clarity, design-confident, occasional dry edge
- **Headspace (circa 2018)** — calm authority, not motivational
- **Patagonia** — mission-confident, never preachy
- **Morning Brew / Finshots** — text-led, sharp observations, accessible
- **Lemonade insurance** — minimalist confidence

### Distinctly NOT

- BYJU's (loud, transactional, exclamation marks)
- Cred (smug-cool, hipster condescension)
- Unacademy (motivational poster energy)
- Duolingo (chaotic, jokey, owl-character)
- LinkedIn-influencer voice ("Today, I learned...")
- Hustle-bro Twitter
- Founder-voice essays ("I think", "I believe")

### The voice in 4 example utterances

These are how the brand would talk if you woke it up at 3am. Use them as tuning forks when a draft sounds off.

1. *"We graded 47,328 mock interview answers. The freeze isn't anxiety. It's the candidate realizing mid-sentence they don't believe what they're saying."*

2. *"There is no good way to say 'I want to be a brand manager because I'm passionate about marketing.' The panel has heard this exact sentence 1,400 times this season."*

3. *"Mock #1342. Question: 'Tell me about a time you led a difficult team.' Watch the answer collapse in real time."*

4. *"January 2023. RBI bans Paytm Payments Bank. Vijay Shekhar Sharma has 48 hours. Three choices were on the table."*

Notice what they share: third-person, specific numbers, no hedging, no exclamation, restrained drama, the brand witnessing — not opining.

## 3. Writing style — embrace

### Voice rules

- **Third person, always.** Brand has no body. No "I", no "we think", no "I believe". "We saw", "we watched", "we graded" is the institutional-witness construction — that's the only first-person plural acceptable.
- **Show, don't claim.** Replace adjectives with numbers. "Many candidates freeze" → "32% of candidates we observed paused for >4 seconds on the third follow-up."
- **Counterintuitive framings.** Lead with the surprising explanation. "The freeze isn't anxiety. It's [actual cause]."
- **Specific verbs over generic ones.** "Said" → "claimed", "dodged", "rehearsed", "stalled".
- **Silence as a content element.** A line break or a single-word slide can do the work of a paragraph.
- **Sentence rhythm.** Mix lengths. Long, observational sentence followed by a short, declarative one. The short one is the punch.
- **Documentary tense.** Present tense for in-the-moment ("She's saying she's data-driven."). Past tense for evidence ("She told us she was data-driven."). Avoid future tense unless making a roadmap claim.
- **Numbers before adjectives.** "Hundreds of candidates" → "847 candidates."
- **Lower-case headlines on social** (LinkedIn / X / IG) for typography-led posts. Title-case feels corporate; lower-case feels considered.

### Structural patterns that work

- **Observation → counter-explanation → evidence → implication.** The Rehearsal essay structure.
- **Setting → crisis → choice → reveal.** The Case Files structure.
- **Question → pause → weak answer → strong answer.** The Brief Roulette structure.
- **Transcript → interjection → collapse.** The Transcript Cuts structure.

### Lexicon to use

- *graded, observed, watched, witnessed, tagged, surfaced, caught*
- *the freeze, the dodge, the stall, the collapse*
- *defensible, articulable, panel-grade, interview-grade*
- *practice layer, defense layer, articulation layer*
- *mock #[number]* (treat each grade as a citation)
- *the third follow-up* (signature DeepProbe construction)
- *under pressure, in real time, on the spot*
- *briefs you read but can't defend*
- *videos you skip, mocks you book once*

## 4. Writing style — refuse

### Phrases never used

- "Crush your interview" / "Nail your PI" / any verb associated with hustle marketing
- "Game-changer", "revolutionary", "next-generation", "cutting-edge"
- "Excited to share", "thrilled to announce", "proud to launch"
- "5 tips", "7 hacks", "the ultimate guide to" (listicle bait)
- "Did you know?", "Here's a hot take", "Unpopular opinion"
- "Let's be honest", "to be honest", "honestly"
- "In today's world", "in this fast-paced world"
- "We're so much more than just an app"
- "Believe in yourself", motivational-poster language
- "Smashing it", "killing it", "crushing it"
- "Founders / co-founders" (no founder narrative — brand-led)
- "Our team", "our mission" (brand is third-person, not collective)
- "AI-powered", "AI-driven" (over-used; we say *DeepProbe* by name)
- Exclamation marks (one per month at most, for genuine surprise only)

### Punctuation rules

- **No exclamation marks.** Exception: rare, surprised observation.
- **Em dashes — sparingly.** Two per post max. They lose force when overused.
- **No ellipsis... drama.** Replace with line breaks or a period.
- **No oxford commas inside brand taglines** (rhythm). Use them in body copy.
- **Period after every CTA.** "Practice this in Rehearsal." (not "Practice this in Rehearsal!")
- **Quotes for candidate speech, italics for product names.** *DeepProbe asked, "What was the difficult moment?"*

### Tone refuses

- **No motivational coaching.** Brand observes; doesn't cheerlead.
- **No founder personality.** No "I think", no "in my experience", no "when I was at IIM". Replace with brand-witness phrasing.
- **No competitive name-calling.** Frame enemies as categories ("summary apps", "the coaching industry"), not as named targets unless quoting a public claim.
- **No "we're better than" comparison.** The brand demonstrates difference, doesn't argue it.
- **No emoji as punctuation.** Emoji allowed only in two places: (a) IG bio, (b) very rare brand-character moments. Never replaces a word.
- **No question-mark hooks** ("Want to know the secret to acing your PI?"). Cheap engagement-bait.

## 6. Audience personas

### Persona A — Indian MBA aspirant (Wave 1 + Wave 3 territory)

- **Stage:** Either CAT result-day to IIM PI (anxiety phase) or IIM Term 4 placement season (performance phase).
- **Emotional state:** Smart but underconfident. Has read InsideIIM, attended IMS / TIME / Career Launcher coaching, taken 3–5 mocks. Still doesn't feel ready.
- **Self-identity:** "I prepared. I just can't perform under pressure."
- **What they share:** Posts that name the freeze, the gap-year defense, the low-CGPA strategy, the recovery-after-bad-mock arc.
- **Where they live:** Instagram (highest), Twitter/X (medium), LinkedIn (rising for MBA-1 / MBA-2 students).
- **What they hate:** Generic motivation, "be confident", coaching-industry condescension.
- **The wedge for them:** *"Your CAT score got you here. It won't get you in."*

### Persona B — Western business-fluency professional (Wave 5 halo)

- **Stage:** 3–10 years into a knowledge-work career. PM, consultant, finance, marketing.
- **Emotional state:** Reads constantly (Blinkist, Personal MBA, HBR, Finshots equivalent). Feels widely informed. Then a board meeting / client call / leadership review hits and they freeze on a concept they "knew" yesterday.
- **Self-identity:** "I read 50 business books and still feel like an impostor in senior conversations."
- **What they share:** Posts that articulate the read-but-can't-apply gap.
- **Where they live:** LinkedIn (highest), Twitter/X (medium for thought-leadership), Instagram (lower).
- **What they hate:** Coursera certifications they'll never finish, MBA-without-MBA scams, summary-app subscriptions they forgot to cancel.
- **The wedge for them:** *"You read Thinking Fast and Slow. Explain System 1 vs System 2 in 30 seconds to a stranger. Right now."*

## 8. Platform grammar

Each platform has a distinct cultural format. The same idea ships differently on each.

### LinkedIn (Rehearsal company page)

**Cultural format:** Long-form thought leadership in brand voice. Native readers expect insight, not virality.

**Post length:** 1,200–1,800 characters in the body (the visible-before-"see more" portion needs to hook). Up to 3,000 characters total.

**Structure:**
- **Line 1:** The hook. A single sentence that earns the next sentence.
- **Lines 2–4:** The setup — observation, data point, or scene.
- **Lines 5–N:** The body. Short paragraphs (1–3 sentences each). White space.
- **Final line:** The brand close — either a tagline, a question for the comments, or a CTA.
- **Hashtags:** 3 max, end of post. Use #MBAplacements, #MBAprep, #InterviewPrep, #DeepProbe, #ConceptBriefs, #IIMprep selectively.

**Visuals:**
- Carousels with 5–10 slides do better than single images on LinkedIn.
- PDFs (as carousel) get higher dwell-time than images.
- Branded data charts perform well.
- No memes; LinkedIn's algorithm penalizes overtly meme-y formats from brand pages.

**Comment voice:** Engage in first 90 minutes after posting. Reply in brand voice — third-person, observational. Never argue. Acknowledge sharp criticisms without defending.

**Tagging:** Tag the company page in CEO/founder reshares only. No friends-of-the-brand tagging — looks engagement-thirsty.

### X (Rehearsal handle)

**Cultural format:** Sharp single observations + occasional threads. Native readers expect velocity, edge, and the unexpected take.

**Post length per format:**
- **Single tweet:** 240–280 chars. Use the full character count if needed.
- **Quote-tweet:** 200 chars of brand voice + the QT'd content does the rest.
- **Thread:** 5–9 tweets. First tweet must be self-contained — it's the only one most people read.
- **Reply (to news / competitor / cultural moment):** 80–180 chars. Restrained.

**Structure (single tweet):**
- Observation in one sentence.
- Optional second sentence as the punch.
- No hashtags unless joining a specific trend.
- No "🧵" thread indicators unless the thread is exceptional.

**Visuals:**
- Single-image quote cards (text on brand background) perform best.
- Screenshots of UI / transcripts get massive shares — text-as-image is X's native viral format.
- GIFs from existing product feature videos work — extract 3-second loops.

**Comment voice:** Engage minimally. The brand replies only to (a) genuinely sharp critique, (b) candidates sharing their own story, (c) clarifying questions. Never DM-baits. Never replies "this!" or "💯".

**Tagging:** Tag InsideIIM, Finshots, igotanoffer only when directly relevant. Never tag candidates without consent.

### Instagram (Rehearsal brand)

**Cultural format:** Visual-first. Carousels and Reels. Captions are secondary — most readers don't see them.

**Post length (caption):** 80–220 characters above the fold. Up to 1,000 if the topic justifies it (rare).

**Structure:**
- **Slide 1 (hook):** Single line. Big typography. Earns the swipe.
- **Slides 2–N (body):** One idea per slide. Each slide can stand alone.
- **Last slide (CTA):** Brand close + "Practice this in Rehearsal."
- **Caption:** Restate the hook in 1 line, then 2–3 sentences expanding, then 5–8 hashtags.

**Visual rules:**
- **Aspect ratio:** 4:5 portrait carousels (1080×1350). Reels at 9:16.
- **Color discipline:** Brand palette only. No stock-image color mismatches.
- **Typography:** Two font weights max per slide. Display weight for hook, regular for body.
- **Slide-to-slide rhythm:** Vary slide density. A dense data slide should be followed by a sparse declarative slide. The reader needs breathing room.

**Comment voice:** Engage in first 2 hours. Heart relevant comments. Reply only when adding value — "Same. We're rolling this out next month." kinds of replies are bad. "What grade would you give yourself on the third follow-up?" is good.

**Hashtags:** 5–8, mix of high-volume (#MBA, #IIM) and niche (#DeepProbe, #ConceptBriefs, #MockInterviewPrep). Place at the end of the caption or in a first comment — both are acceptable.

**Stories:** Mostly re-shares from posts + behind-the-scenes (product UI clips, data observations). Polls and questions in Stories drive DM conversation.

## 9. The four content pillars

Each pillar gets the full anatomy: strategic role, hook formula, format-per-platform, voice notes, visual rules, what to avoid, and 2–3 sample posts.

### Pillar 1 — The Articulation Gap

**Strategic role:** The institutional-witness wedge. Establishes that Rehearsal has watched more mock interviews than any single coach or institution, and reports back patterns. Sets up the other three pillars intellectually.

**Hook formula:**
> *"We graded [N] [thing]. The [pattern] wasn't [obvious-explanation]. It was [counterintuitive-explanation]."*

**Variations:**
- *"We tagged [behavior] across [N] [units]. Here's what we found."*
- *"The most common [phrase / answer / mistake] in our dataset is [specific]. The most common panel reaction is [specific]."*
- *"After [N] mocks, one pattern doesn't go away: [pattern]."*

**Voice notes specific to this pillar:**
- Lead with the data, not the framing.
- Specific numbers, not "many" / "most" / "a lot".
- The implication is the second beat, never the first.
- No prescription in the post itself — let the reader feel the pattern. The product CTA can sit at the end.

**Visual rules:**
- Brand color background, single data point in display typography.
- Charts only when the data is sharp enough to defend.
- Never use stock-photo "businessperson looking concerned".

**Format anatomy by platform:**

**LinkedIn (text + optional carousel):**
```
[Line 1 — the surprising data point]

[Lines 2–3 — what we expected vs. what we found]

[Lines 4–6 — the implication, with one example]

[Final line — the brand close or question]
```

**X (single tweet or 3-tweet thread):**
- Tweet 1: the data observation, self-contained.
- Tweet 2 (optional): the counter-explanation.
- Tweet 3 (optional): one specific example.

**Instagram (5-slide carousel):**
1. Display-typography hook with the data point
2. The expected explanation (subtler typography)
3. The actual explanation (high-contrast)
4. One specific example
5. Brand close

**What to avoid:**
- Vague claims ("Many candidates struggle with confidence" — refused).
- Coaching language ("Here's how to fix this" — refused; the brand observes, doesn't prescribe).
- Cherry-picked data without sample size.

**Sample posts:**

**Sample 1.1 — LinkedIn:**
> We graded 47,328 mock interview answers across the 2025–26 IIM season.
>
> The single most-said phrase was "I want to be a brand manager because I'm passionate about marketing."
>
> The most common panel response was silence.
>
> "Passionate" is not a credential. It's a placeholder for the credential the candidate hasn't built yet. The panel hears 1,400 versions of it in a recruiting window. By the second week, they stop listening to anything that follows the word.
>
> The candidates who get the offer don't sound more confident. They sound more specific.
>
> Practice this in Rehearsal.
>
> #MBAplacements #InterviewPrep #DeepProbe

**Sample 1.2 — X (single tweet):**
> Most-said phrase in our 2025-26 mock dataset:
>
> "I want to be a brand manager because I'm passionate about marketing."
>
> Most common panel response: silence.
>
> Passionate isn't a credential. It's a placeholder for the credential the candidate hasn't built.

**Sample 1.3 — Instagram carousel:**

- **Slide 1:** "47,328 mock answers graded." (display typography, brand background)
- **Slide 2:** "Most-said phrase:" / *"I want to be a brand manager because I'm passionate about marketing."* (quote treatment)
- **Slide 3:** "Most common panel response:" / [empty slide with single word] *"Silence."*
- **Slide 4:** "Passionate isn't a credential. It's a placeholder for the credential the candidate hasn't built yet."
- **Slide 5:** Brand close — Rehearsal logo + "Practice this in Rehearsal." + soft CTA to bio link.

### Pillar 2 — Brief Roulette

**Strategic role:** The daily content engine. Directly substitutes Finshots' content-as-information positioning by reframing the same topics as content-as-defense-material. Builds compounding distribution through daily ritual.

**Hook formula:**
> *"[Today's news event or business concept]. If a panel asked about this tomorrow, here's what they'd grade you on."*

**Variations:**
- *"This week's defense question: [topic]. 90 seconds. Go."*
- *"You read [Finshots / HBR / news source] this morning. Can you defend it in a meeting this afternoon?"*
- *"[Topic] in 60 seconds. Start: [setup line]. End: [implication line]. What goes in the middle?"*

**Voice notes specific to this pillar:**
- The brand is the panel, not the candidate.
- Frame the question, then the rubric, then the example.
- The "strong answer" should be 3–5 sentences max — fits on one slide.
- Topic selection: lean toward business/finance/policy with India relevance. Avoid pure-celebrity topics.

**Topic source priorities (rotate):**
1. Finshots' last 7 days (direct substitution play — they've validated the topic has interest)
2. The week's biggest Indian business news (LiveMint / ET / Bloomberg India)
3. Concept Briefs already shipped on the platform (cross-link to product)
4. Wave 1–3 ranked SEO pages (cross-link to Rehearsal-owned content)
5. Quarterly evergreens (Big Four, Indus Basin Treaty, IBC, etc. — recurring panel topics)

**Visual rules:**
- News-headline slide (real masthead acceptable for fair-use editorial commentary; AI-generated abstract illustration safer).
- The "pause" slide is essential — single word ("Pause." or "Could you?") on solid color.
- Strong-answer slide uses a different typography weight than weak-answer.

**Format anatomy by platform:**

**Instagram (5-slide carousel) — 3×/week:**
1. Topic headline + illustration ("Karnataka bike taxis. 90 seconds.")
2. Pause slide ("Could you defend this in PI?")
3. Weak answer in candidate-typography ("Karnataka is, uh, banning bike taxis, which is bad for the gig economy…")
4. Strong answer in evaluator-typography (3 specific points)
5. Brand close + "Practice this in Rehearsal."

**X (daily):**
- Single tweet: question + the framing only. *"Today's defense question: The Karnataka bike taxi policy. You have 90 seconds. What's your first sentence?"*
- Optional reply with the strong-answer rubric (drives engagement).

**LinkedIn (1×/week, long-form):**
- Full essay on one Brief Roulette topic.
- Structure: setup → why the panel would ask → three answer tiers (weak / acceptable / strong) → what each reveals about the candidate.
- 1,500–1,800 characters.

**What to avoid:**
- Pure politics. Stay on policy/economics/business decisions.
- Religious or communal topics — never.
- Mocking the weak answer. The "weak answer" voiceover should feel like a real candidate, not a parody.
- Over-prescribing the "right" answer. There are tiers, not a single right.

**Sample posts:**

**Sample 2.1 — X:**
> Today's defense question:
>
> The Karnataka High Court ruling that bike taxis cannot operate under the existing Motor Vehicles Act framework.
>
> You have 90 seconds. What's your first sentence?

**Sample 2.2 — Instagram carousel:**

- **Slide 1:** "Karnataka bike taxis. 90 seconds." (with abstract illustration of a stylized motorcycle silhouette)
- **Slide 2:** "Could you defend this in PI?" (single line, brand color)
- **Slide 3:** *"So, uh, Karnataka is banning bike taxis, which I think is bad for the gig economy because Rapido and others were employing thousands of people…"* (candidate-voice typography)
- **Slide 4:** "Strong answer:" / "1. The legal frame — bike taxis don't fit the MV Act commercial-vehicle definition. 2. The economic stakes — Rapido / Uber Moto employ ~250K riders in Bangalore. 3. The policy precedent — Maharashtra, Delhi face the same gap. 4. The real question for the panel — is this a court interpretation or a policy vacuum?"
- **Slide 5:** Brand close — "Practice this in Rehearsal."

**Sample 2.3 — LinkedIn:**
> The Karnataka High Court ruling that bike taxis cannot operate under the existing Motor Vehicles Act framework is one of the highest-probability MBA PI questions this season.
>
> Here's why the panel would ask it. And here's how three candidates we observed defended it last week.
>
> Candidate A opened with: "I think it's bad for the gig economy." Panel let her keep going for 40 seconds before the third follow-up: "What specifically is bad?" She didn't have an answer.
>
> Candidate B opened with: "The court is interpreting the Motor Vehicles Act strictly." Better. The panel pressed: "Why now? Why Karnataka?" She had a frame: "Because the regulatory gap was always there. The court just made the gap visible."
>
> Candidate C went further: "This isn't a court decision. It's a policy vacuum that the court is being asked to fill. The panel that needs to act is the central government — and they won't, because rural employment data is on the wrong side of the bike-taxi argument."
>
> All three know the news. Only one is panel-grade.
>
> Practice this in Rehearsal.
>
> #MBAprep #PolicyAnalysis #IIMprep

### Pillar 3 — Case Files

**Strategic role:** Strategy-debate driver. Decision cases get the highest comment density of any LinkedIn format. Showcases the brand's documentary narration voice (matches the in-product experience). Leverages already-shipped IP from the case registry.

**Hook formula:**
> *"[Date]. [Crisis hits]. [CEO] has [time]. [Stakes]."*

**Variations:**
- *"[CEO] is about to make the [defining decision of their career]. The room has [N] people in it. None of them agree."*
- *"Three choices were on the table. The wrong one would [consequence]. The right one would [consequence]."*

**Voice notes specific to this pillar:**
- Documentary narration. Past tense for the recount, present tense for the dramatic in-scene moments.
- Never editorialize. The brand reports what happened, then poses the question.
- The "What would you do?" prompt is at the decision-point slide, not at the end.
- Twist at the end is mandatory — what most people miss about the decision.

**Case inventory (as of 2026-05):**
- GBA 2026-001: Paytm at the Fintech Crossroads (Jan 2023, RBI ban)
- GBA 2026-002: Boeing 737 MAX — The Grounding Decision (2019)
- RHL 2026-004: Johnson & Johnson at the Tylenol Crossroads (1982)
- GBA 2026-003: Zomato and Blinkit — The Quick Commerce Bet (2022)

Use each case at most 1×/month per platform.

**Visual rules:**
- AI-generated illustrations (Nano Banana / Midjourney) acceptable and encouraged for cinematic feel.
- Style: high-contrast, restrained color palette. Think A24 film posters — moody but not melodramatic.
- Avoid using real CEO photos without licensing — abstract silhouettes / illustrated figures preferred.
- Date stamps and location stamps in monospace typography lend the documentary feel.

**Format anatomy by platform:**

**Instagram (7–10 slide carousel) — 1×/week:**
1. Title card: "Mock #001: Paytm at the Crossroads — January 2023"
2. Setting: AI illustration + date/location overlay
3. Crisis: the central event in one sentence
4. Stakes: what's at risk, in 2–3 numbers
5. Choices: three options, each as a slide or a tri-panel
6. Decision-pause: "What would you do?"
7. Reveal: what they actually did
8. Twist: "What most people miss about this decision is —"

### Pillar 4 — Transcript Cuts

**Strategic role:** The cinéma-vérité pillar without needing video. Pure typography. Real anonymized mock-answer transcripts dissected with DeepProbe's actual interjections. Highest emotional density per slide. Lowest production cost. Most likely to break out virally because awkward-transcript screenshots are a proven format (the "this you?" genre).

**Hook formula:**
> *"Mock #[N]. Question: '[panel question].' Watch the answer collapse."*

**Variations:**
- *"Transcript: [candidate ID]. The freeze starts at second [N]."*
- *"Three candidates. Same question. Watch where each one breaks."*
- *"DeepProbe asked the third follow-up. Here's what 100 candidates said next."*

**Voice notes specific to this pillar:**
- Anonymize aggressively. Replace any name with "the candidate". Change identifying details (school, role, sector) to a category. Confirm with legal before posting anything from real session data.
- Show the candidate sympathetically. The brand isn't mocking — the brand is reporting. Tone is forensic, not gleeful.
- The DeepProbe interjection is the structural turn. Format it visually distinct.
- The collapse is the punch — let it land. No commentary needed.

**Data source rules:**
- Real transcripts (with anonymization) are first-choice. Higher trust.
- Composed transcripts (from observed patterns) are acceptable as a fallback when real data is too identifying. **Always label composed transcripts as "composite" in small text on the final slide.**
- Never publish a transcript that could plausibly identify a specific candidate. When in doubt, compose.

**Visual rules:**
- Monospace typography for the candidate's words (gives it the documentary-record feel).
- DeepProbe interjections in brand-color sans-serif (different visual register).
- Black or near-black backgrounds. White or off-white text. No decorative elements.
- Pacing: short slides. Don't put a whole transcript on one slide. Break at the pause moments.

**Format anatomy by platform:**

**Instagram (4–6 slide carousel) — 2×/week:**
1. Title slide: "Mock #1342 / Question: 'Tell me about a time you led a difficult team.'"
2. Transcript slide: the candidate's first 2–3 sentences (verbatim feel, anonymized)
3. DeepProbe interjection slide: the third follow-up question, in brand color
4. Candidate collapse: 1–2 lines, often *"…I'd have to think about that."* or similar
5. Brand close: "This is what we train you out of. Practice in Rehearsal."

**X (single image + tweet) — 2×/week:**
- Single image: the transcript with DeepProbe interjection inline, formatted as a "screenshot" of a hypothetical app — but it's actually a brand-typography composition.
- Tweet caption: 1–2 lines framing the post. *"Most candidates handle the first question. The third follow-up is where the rehearsal happens."*

**LinkedIn (long-form, 1×/week):**
- Three transcripts in a single post. Each anonymized.
- Show the pattern across them.
- Structure: opening observation → transcript 1 → transcript 2 → transcript 3 → the shared pattern → brand close.
- Approximately 2,000 characters.

**What to avoid:**
- Identifiable details (school, exact role, recent ranks).
- Mocking tone. The brand is forensic, never cruel.
- Composite transcripts that imply real data (mislabeling is a trust violation).
- Excessive use of any single transcript across platforms (cross-post fatigue).

**Sample posts:**

**Sample 4.1 — Instagram carousel:**

## 10. Visual design system

### Color palette (use Rehearsal brand kit)

- **Primary background:** Deep charcoal / near-black (carousels), or pure white (LinkedIn data charts)
- **Brand accent:** Orange (consistent with the website — orange-400, orange-500 range)
- **Typography color:** Off-white (#F5F1EA-ish) on dark, near-black on light
- **DeepProbe interjection color:** Brand accent orange — distinct from candidate body text
- **Data viz colors:** Restrained palette of 3–4 colors max. No rainbow gradients.

### Typography hierarchy

- **Display weight (hooks, titles):** Brand serif or display sans, very large (80–140pt on a 1080×1350 canvas)
- **Body weight:** Brand sans-serif, medium (32–48pt for IG carousels)
- **Monospace (transcripts, dates, mock IDs):** Brand monospace
- **Caption typography (small disclaimers, "composite" labels):** 18–22pt, low contrast

### Aspect ratios

- **Instagram feed:** 4:5 portrait (1080×1350)
- **Instagram Reels (when used):** 9:16 (1080×1920)
- **LinkedIn images / carousels:** 1.91:1 (1200×627) for single images, 4:5 for carousels (LinkedIn now supports portrait)
- **LinkedIn carousel PDF:** A4 landscape (works best for native PDF carousel)
- **X images:** 16:9 (1200×675) or 1:1 (1080×1080) for quote cards

### Slide-to-slide rhythm rules

A 5-slide IG carousel should NOT have 5 same-density slides. Pattern that works:
- Slide 1: dense (hook + visual)
- Slide 2: sparse (single declarative line)
- Slide 3: dense (data or transcript)
- Slide 4: sparse (the punch)
- Slide 5: medium (brand close)

The eye needs breathing room. Two consecutive text-heavy slides lose readers.

## 11. AI illustration guidelines (for Case Files + abstract Brief Roulette imagery)

**Tools:** Nano Banana (Gemini), Midjourney, or equivalent.

**Style brief to feed the generator:**
> *Cinematic editorial illustration. Restrained color palette. High contrast. A24 film poster aesthetic. Documentary realism with a slight stylization. No text, no logos, no recognizable real people. Mood: contemplative, suspended, like a frozen frame from a thriller.*

**Subject types that work:**
- Architectural silhouettes (offices at night, conference rooms with one lit chair)
- Abstract object metaphors (a single document on an empty desk, a phone face-down)
- Stylized illustrated figures (no face detail, gestural only)
- Moody landscapes that anchor a location

**Subject types to avoid:**
- Real CEO likenesses (legal + ethical risk)
- Stock-photo "businesspeople smiling at laptop"
- Cartoonish or over-stylized characters
- Anything that looks AI-generated (extra fingers, melted backgrounds, etc.)

**Quality bar:** If you wouldn't put the illustration on a magazine cover, don't put it in a post. Regenerate.

## 14. Honesty guardrails (non-negotiable)

These are hard rules. A draft that violates any of these does not ship.

### Product claims

- **NEVER** claim a feature that isn't shipped. Capture-loop features (voice notes, Chrome extension, prep-memory) live ONLY on /roadmap.
- **DeepProbe is shipped** — claim freely.
- **Concept Briefs are shipped** — claim freely.
- **Mock interview grading is shipped** — claim freely.
- **Email capture for free tools** — claim only when the tool surface is live.

### Data claims

- **Numbers must be real or labeled as illustrative.** "We graded 47,328 answers" must reflect actual platform data within ±10%. If using a hypothetical number, label it ("In an internal sample of ~500 answers…").
- **Surveys / studies cited must link to source.** Don't invent statistics.
- **Sample sizes must be disclosed** if the claim depends on them.

### Transcript handling (Transcript Cuts pillar)

Pre-publish anonymization checklist — every transcript must pass ALL of these:

1. ☐ No candidate name, even partially
2. ☐ No specific school (replace with "tier-1 IIM" or "consulting firm" category)
3. ☐ No specific employer (replace with "FMCG company" or category)
4. ☐ No specific dates that could identify a session
5. ☐ No phrases unique to a single candidate (idiomatic English, specific stories that could ID them)
6. ☐ Reviewed by 1 other person before publishing
7. ☐ If composed (not real data), labeled "composite" in small text on the final slide

If even one box is unchecked, the post does not ship. When in doubt, compose.

### Competitor mentions

- **Never mention a competitor by name in a dunking context.**
- **Frame competitive substitution as category-level**, not target-level.
- Acceptable: *"Summary apps don't make you smarter."* — Refused: *"Blinkist doesn't make you smarter."*
- Acceptable: *"Finshots is the news. Rehearsal is the practice."* — borderline acceptable because it's positioning, not dunking. Test by asking: would Finshots' team take legal action? If yes, rephrase.

### Tone guardrails

- **Never demean candidates.** Mock the system, not the people.
- **Never demean coaches or coaching as individuals.** Critique industry practices, not human professionals.
- **Never make claims about specific people's intelligence, capability, or character.**
- **Never use AI-generated likenesses of real people** in illustrations.

## 16. Pre-publish quality checklist

Every draft must pass this gate before scheduling:

### Voice check
- ☐ No first-person singular ("I", "me", "my")
- ☐ No "we believe", "we think" (replace with "we observed", "we graded", "we watched")
- ☐ No exclamation marks (unless rare, justified surprise)
- ☐ No "5 tips", "X reasons" listicle structures
- ☐ No motivational coaching language
- ☐ No founder personality intrusions
- ☐ Tone matches the calm-documentary-observer reference utterances

### Content check
- ☐ Specific numbers, not "many" / "most" / "lots"
- ☐ At least one concrete example (not abstract claims only)
- ☐ Counterintuitive framing in the lead (not the obvious explanation)
- ☐ The brand close is exactly one tagline from the approved bank
- ☐ No competitor mentioned in dunking context

### Honesty check
- ☐ No claim about an unshipped feature
- ☐ Data figures are real or labeled illustrative
- ☐ If transcript content, all 7 anonymization checks pass
- ☐ All third-party sources linked

### Platform fit check
- ☐ LinkedIn: hook earns the "see more" click; 3 hashtags max
- ☐ X: first tweet self-contained; thread only if structurally required
- ☐ IG: hook on Slide 1 earns the swipe; carousel has rhythm variance; 5–8 hashtags

### Visual check
- ☐ Brand palette only
- ☐ Typography hierarchy clear (no more than 2 weights per slide)
- ☐ Aspect ratio correct for platform
- ☐ AI illustrations meet the magazine-cover bar
- ☐ No accidental brand logos / watermarks from generation tools

If any box is unchecked, the post does not ship.

## 18. Comment and DM response voice

### Comment responses

**General rules:**
- Reply in the first 90 minutes after posting (algorithm rewards engagement velocity)
- Use the brand voice — third-person, observational
- Never argue. If criticism is sharp, acknowledge ("Fair point. We didn't address X — adding that to next week's post.")

**A user critiques the post:**
> *"That's a sharper point than the post made. The freeze is multi-causal — we focused on one cause this week. Worth a separate post on the others — adding it."*

**A user from a coaching company / competitor pushes back:**
> *"Appreciate the read. Different models for different stages — we focus on the practice layer that fits between the mocks people book once and the actual interview. Happy to share data on what we see."*

### DM responses

DMs are 1:1, lower stakes for brand voice but still brand-aligned. Templates:

**A user asks about a feature:**

- *"DM us to learn more"* — looks desperate; CTA goes to the website
- *"We hear you 💙"* — saccharine
- *"Stay tuned…"* — vague engagement-bait

## 19. Crisis and edge-case protocol

### If a post goes unusually viral (positive)

- **Don't panic-publish a follow-up.** The brand voice is patient.
- **Pin the post on the platform** where it's blowing up.
- **Reply to the top 10 comments** in brand voice.
- **Save the metrics** for the post-mortem (what topic, what format, what hour — learn the lesson).
- **Resist the urge to caption-edit mid-flight** — looks insecure.

### If a post draws criticism (negative)

- **Hold for 30 minutes before responding.** Almost no online critique requires a same-minute reply.
- **If the critique is factual** (claimed data is wrong, claim misrepresents): acknowledge in a reply, edit the post if the platform allows, note the correction.
- **If the critique is interpretive** (someone disagrees with the framing): one reply maximum, in brand voice, acknowledging the disagreement without conceding the frame.
- **If the critique is hostile / bad-faith:** don't reply. Let other commenters defend if they choose. Brand doesn't get into flame wars.

### If a candidate complains about a Transcript Cut

- **Take the post down immediately.** Do not negotiate.
- **Apologize directly** via DM if contact is available.
- **Internal review:** how did the anonymization checklist fail?
- **Public statement only if the original post had >10K reach.** Keep it short, factual.

### If something contradicts the master descriptor

- The descriptor is canonical. Posts that contradict it (e.g., implying Rehearsal is "just like Blinkist") get rewritten or pulled.

### Sample C — Case Files on LinkedIn

> January 2023. The RBI bans Paytm Payments Bank from onboarding new customers.
>
> Vijay Shekhar Sharma has 48 hours before the markets open Monday.
>
> Three choices on the table.
>
> One: Issue an immediate, defensive statement. Reassure investors that operations continue. Hope the stock holds.
>
> Two: Take the offensive. Frame the RBI action as a misunderstanding, hint at regulatory overreach, mobilize the founder narrative. Sharma had done this before — Demonetization 2016 had been a tailwind.
>
> Three: Stay silent. Let lawyers respond formally on Monday. Don't speak as the founder until the technical answer is ready.
>
> Each choice signals something different to the market — and to the RBI.
>
> What would you do?
>
> (Read the room before answering. The audience for this statement isn't retail investors. It's three people in Mumbai who decide whether the ban gets lifted in 60 days or 600.)
>
> Sharma chose two. By Tuesday, the stock was down 27%. By Friday, the regulatory window had narrowed further.
>
> What most people miss about this decision: the choice wasn't between defense and offense. It was between speaking to investors and speaking to the regulator. They needed different things. Sharma tried to do both in the same statement. Neither audience was satisfied.
>
> More cases like this in Rehearsal.
>
> #CaseStudy #DecisionMaking #MBAlearning
